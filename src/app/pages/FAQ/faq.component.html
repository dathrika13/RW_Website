3.MANDATES

• WHICH COUNTRIES HAVE IMPLEMENTED ESG DISCLOSURE LAWS?

As of today, several countries have implemented ESG disclosure regulations. For instance, Australia requires listed companies to disclose material exposure to environmental or social risks and how they manage or intend to manage those risks. Similarly, the European Union has introduced the Sustainability Finance Disclosures Regulation (SFDR) and the EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) to enhance ESG transparency. In the United States, California has passed SB253/261 climate disclosure laws. Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) has mandated Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting (BRSR) format for listed entities. Additionally, many countries have formed task forces and working groups to explore implementing mandatory ESG disclosure regulations in the near future.

• WHAT IS EU CSRD AND WHEN IS IT EFFECTIVE?

The EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) is a new regulation that requires large companies and listed companies (except micro-enterprises) to report on sustainability matters. The CSRD aims to enhance the transparency and comparability of sustainability information. It enters into force on January 5, 2023, and will be phased in starting from January 1, 2024, for certain large EU and EU-listed companies

• ARE THERE ANY PENALTIES FOR NOT COMPLYING WITH CSRD?

Yes, there are penalties for non-compliance with the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD). Companies that fail to comply with the CSRD may face legal penalties, fines, and damage to their reputation. Additionally, each EU member state will determine its own penalties for non-compliance, which may include fines up to €10 million or 5% of a company's global annual turnover.

• WHAT ARE SB253/261 CLIMATE DISCLOSURE LAWS?

California's SB 253 and SB 261, signed into law on October 7, 2023, require companies with annual revenues exceeding $1 billion and $500 million respectively, that do business in California, to disclose greenhouse gas emissions and climate-related financial risks. Disclosures begin in 2026 and 2027 respectively, with penalties for non-compliance up to $500,000 and $50,000 respectively. These laws aim to promote climate action and transparency.
